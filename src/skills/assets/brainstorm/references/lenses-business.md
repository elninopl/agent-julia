# Business lenses

Original distillations of well-known thinking tools. Pick 1–2 per session, by
fit — never run all of them. Name the lens when you use it. Each lens: when it
applies, the questions to run, what it should produce.

## Strategy kernel

**When:** "what's our strategy for X", any plan that smells like a wish list.

1. **Diagnosis** — what is the ONE critical obstacle or force at play? Not a
   list of ten; the one that, if wrong, invalidates everything else.
2. **Guiding policy** — the overall approach that addresses the diagnosis.
   Run the *negation test*: if the opposite of your policy is something no
   sane competitor would ever choose ("we will be inefficient"), the policy is
   empty fluff. A real policy has a sensible opposite you're rejecting.
3. **Coherent actions** — 3–5 concrete moves, each with an owner and a date,
   that reinforce each other. Actions that don't serve the policy get cut.

Also run: the *paste test* — could a competitor paste your strategy into their
deck unchanged? If yes, it's not a strategy. **Produces:** a one-page kernel
plus an explicit no-list (what you will NOT do).

## Value curve (ERRC)

**When:** ideation about products, offers, differentiation; "how do we stand out".

Sketch the factors your market competes on and where everyone clusters. Then
force four lists: **Eliminate** (factors the industry takes for granted that
add no real value), **Reduce** (below standard — where you're over-serving),
**Raise** (well above standard), **Create** (factors the industry never
offered). The discipline is in Eliminate/Reduce — a curve that only raises and
creates is a cost structure, not a strategy. **Produces:** an ERRC grid and one
sentence naming who the new offer serves that the current market ignores.

## Jobs to be done

**When:** feature ideas, "what should we build", understanding demand.

- What progress is the customer trying to make, in what circumstance? Phrase
  it without your product's name in the sentence.
- What do they "hire" today for that job — including non-obvious competitors
  (a spreadsheet, a spouse, doing nothing)?
- Map the four forces: what pushes them from the current way, what pulls them
  to the new one, what anxieties hold them back, what habits keep them put.
  Demand exists when push+pull outweigh anxiety+habit — you can work on all
  four, not just pull.

**Produces:** a job statement and the force map for the strongest option.

## Evidence check (customer-conversation hygiene)

**When:** any option justified by "customers want it" or "people told me".

- Was the evidence about their PAST behavior and specifics, or opinions and
  hypotheticals? "Would you use…" answers are worth nothing; "when did you
  last…, what did it cost you" answers are gold.
- Did anyone give up something real — money, time, reputation, an intro? A
  compliment is not a commitment.
- Who exactly said it, and are they the buyer? Enthusiasm from non-buyers is
  the most common false positive.

**Produces:** each front-runner's evidence graded strong / weak / anecdote —
and, where weak, the cheapest conversation or test that would upgrade it.

## Willingness to pay

**When:** pricing, packaging, "is this worth building".

- Have you asked what the customer would pay — before building, not after?
  What price would make it feel expensive-but-worth-it vs. a rip-off?
- What is the alternative's full cost to them (money + hassle)? Your ceiling.
- Which of your option's features drive that willingness, and which are
  expensive decoration? Would a cheaper option with only the drivers sell?
- Is the model (one-off, subscription, usage) matched to how value arrives?

**Produces:** a price hypothesis with its riskiest assumption named, and what
gets cut because nobody would pay for it.

## Appetite & shaping

**When:** scoping any project; "how big should this be".

Flip the default question. Not "how long will this take?" but "how much time
is this WORTH?" — fix the appetite (e.g. two weeks, one quarter), then shape a
solution that fits. If it doesn't fit, cut scope, not quality, and not the
deadline. Sketch the solution fat-marker coarse: main flows only, no detail
that would prejudge implementation. Name explicitly what's OUT of scope and
which rabbit holes you're declining to enter. **Produces:** an appetite, a
coarse shape, and a no-list.

## Positioning

**When:** "how do we talk about this", launch prep, confused-market symptoms.

Work the chain in order: what would customers use if you didn't exist (real
alternatives, including "nothing") → what do you have that those lack (unique
attributes) → what value do those attributes enable, for whom does that value
matter most → what market category makes that value obvious? Category last,
not first — picking the category first is how products end up compared to the
wrong rivals on the wrong criteria. **Produces:** best-fit customer, top-3
value points, and the category frame to lead with.

## Cold start

**When:** anything with network effects, marketplaces, communities, referrals.

- What is the smallest ATOMIC network that is self-sustaining — the minimum
  set of people/sides in one place at one time for the product to work at all?
  (Often shockingly small: one team, one building, one city block.)
- Which side is harder to get, and what would you do ONLY for them, even
  unscalably?
- What does the product do for user #1 when the network is empty — is there
  single-player value while the network fills?

**Produces:** a definition of the atomic network and the plan to fill exactly
one of them before generalizing.
