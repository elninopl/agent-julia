# Life & personal lenses

For decisions about how to live, work, and spend time — moves, career shifts,
commitments, habits, money-adjacent life choices. Pick 1–2 per session, name
the lens. Tone matters more here: no smugness, no life-coaching filler. If the
topic touches health, grief, or relationships under strain, drop cleverness
entirely and slow down.

## Fear-setting

**When:** the user wants something but keeps not doing it; fear is the blocker.

Three columns for the feared action:

1. **Define** — the worst realistic outcomes, specifically. "It fails" is not
   specific; "I lose the €X and have to tell Y" is.
2. **Prevent** — for each, what reduces its likelihood?
3. **Repair** — if it happens anyway, how would you recover, and who has
   recovered from the same?

Then the half everyone skips: the **cost of inaction** at 6 months, 2 years,
10 years — emotionally, financially, physically. Fear-setting usually reveals
the status quo as the riskier option; sometimes it confirms the fear is sound.
Both are wins. **Produces:** the three columns plus the inaction ledger.

## Inversion

**When:** any goal-shaped topic; especially good when the user is stuck on
"how do I achieve X".

Ask the opposite question: "what would guarantee X fails?" or "how would I
make my life around this reliably miserable?" List the answers, then check
which ingredients are already present in the current plan or life. Avoiding
stupidity is more tractable than seeking brilliance, and the failure list is
usually more actionable than the success list. **Produces:** the
guaranteed-failure list and which items are live today.

## Regret minimization

**When:** big, identity-level forks — career, moving, having/not having,
starting/quitting.

Project to age 80 and look back: which choice would you regret NOT having
tried? This lens deliberately overweights bold moves and underweights
switching costs — say so. Pair it with one grounding question: "what would
this choice cost the people who depend on you?" so it doesn't become a
permission slip for recklessness. **Produces:** which option the 80-year-old
picks, and whether the grounding question changes it.

## 10/10/10

**When:** the decision is emotionally hot right now; anger, hurt, excitement.

How will you feel about each option in 10 minutes, 10 months, 10 years? The
lens separates the emotional spike from the long arc — most hot decisions look
different at 10 months and identical at 10 years, which tells you how much the
choice actually matters. **Produces:** the three-horizon read per option, and
often a downgrade of the decision's perceived stakes.

## Second-order effects

**When:** an option looks obviously good; lifestyle changes with compounding
consequences (moving, remote work, a dog, a side project).

For each front-runner: "and then what?" — twice. First-order: the immediate
result. Second-order: what that result causes — in time, relationships,
energy, money, identity — a year in. Good options often have unpleasant first
order and compounding second order (exercise, saving, hard conversations);
bad ones invert that (comfort now, erosion later). **Produces:** a
two-step consequence chain per option, flagging any comfort-now/erosion-later
pattern.

## One-way vs two-way doors

**When:** stakes feel high but nobody has checked reversibility.

Classify each option: can it be undone, at what cost, within what window?
Two-way doors (reversible cheaply) deserve fast, lightweight decisions —
deciding slowly IS the mistake. One-way doors (irreversible or very costly to
reverse) justify the full process and deep mode. Many options can be converted
into two-way doors with a small design change: a trial period, a sabbatical
instead of a resignation, renting before buying. Look for that conversion.
**Produces:** a door classification per option and any cheap reversibility
conversions found.

## Via negativa

**When:** the user wants to ADD something to an already full life; overwhelm,
"I need a system", too many commitments.

Before adding, subtract: what would you remove to make room, and what would
happen if you removed the most draining item and added nothing? Improvement by
removal is more reliable and cheaper than improvement by addition, and the
question "what do I stop?" has usually never been asked. **Produces:** a
stop-doing candidate list, ranked by energy reclaimed per unit of guilt.

## Energy audit

**When:** dissatisfaction without a clear cause; "something's off";
prioritizing between lives rather than tasks.

Walk a typical recent week: which activities and people reliably gave energy,
which reliably drained it? Then check each option on the table against the
ledger — does it add givers or add drains? A choice that scores brilliantly on
money and status but staffs your week with drains is a bad choice measured in
the only currency that compounds. **Produces:** the giver/drain ledger and a
re-score of the options against it.
